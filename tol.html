Tower of London in Virtual Reality

Description

The frontal lobe is a large cortical region involved in brain functions such as executive skills, which subsume attention, reasoning, planning, and impulse management (Scott & Schoenberg, 2011). Executive skills are often impaired in psychological conditions like autism spectrum disorder and attention deficit hyperactivity disorder, along with degenerative brain disorders like stroke and dementia (Cleveland Clinic, 2024). Tower tasks are assessments that require the user to reach a goal position in the fewest number of moves, a task calling upon planning abilities. Current popular tower tasks include the Tower of Hanoi and the Tower of London, both with various adaptations. The Tower of Hanoi requires the user to move all disks from the left to the right peg in the fewest number of moves with two constraints: (1) the user may not place a smaller disk under a larger one and (2) only one disk can be moved at a time. The Tower of London aimed to operationalize the difficulty of the problems in the Tower of Hanoi to more precisely assess planning in people with frontal lobe difficulties. All of the balls are the same size in the Tower of London, and only one ball can be moved at a time. The pegs are also of different sizes, holding three, two, and one balls, respectively. The original test developed by Shallice (1982) comprised 12 problems, with the differing starting and ending positions tempering the difficulty of each problem. However, one problem with the growing popularization of these Tower Tasks is the variability of the versions employed by psychologists to test people with differing disorders, presuming interchangeability among them.
